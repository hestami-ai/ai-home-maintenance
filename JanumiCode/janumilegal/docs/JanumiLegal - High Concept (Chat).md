# 5\. The legal agent roles

A plausible JanumiLegal role architecture:

## Orchestrator

Controls matter workflow, decomposes tasks, routes to agents, enforces review gates.

## Client Intent Steward

Preserves the client’s objective, business posture, negotiation preferences, and risk tolerance.

This is the legal version of your JanumiCode Intent Steward. It prevents the system from producing technically plausible legal work that does not serve the client’s actual goal.

## Legal Research Agent

Finds relevant authority, but does not finalize conclusions without verification.

## Authority Verifier

Checks citations, quotations, jurisdiction, precedential weight, currency, and whether a case/statute actually supports the proposition.

This is one of the most important legal agents.

## Adverse Authority Agent

Searches for cases, regulations, clauses, or facts that weaken the proposed answer.

This is the legal version of a counterfactual or red-team agent.

## Drafting Agent

Produces memos, clauses, policies, filings, letters, or summaries.

## Consistency Agent

Checks defined terms, internal contradictions, cross-references, dates, party names, obligations, and remedies.

## Risk Classifier

Labels risks by severity, likelihood, reversibility, financial impact, regulatory exposure, litigation exposure, and reputational risk.

## Privilege and Confidentiality Guard

Identifies privileged content, sensitive client facts, work product, PII, trade secrets, and materials that should not be sent to external systems.

## Attorney Review Agent

Packages the work for human legal review.

This agent does not “approve.” It prepares a concise review packet:

Decision needed:

Approve use of fallback indemnity clause.

Why it matters:

Vendor’s proposed clause shifts all third-party claims to customer.

Recommended position:

Reject as drafted; propose mutual indemnity limited to each party’s breach, negligence, or willful misconduct.

Open legal uncertainty:

State enforceability of consequential damages exclusion should be reviewed.

## Unsticking Agent

Used when the workflow is blocked by ambiguity, missing facts, or conflicting authorities.

Instead of guessing, it creates an escalation packet:

Blocked because:

The governing documents do not clearly authorize the proposed fine.

Needed from human:

1\. Confirm association type: HOA, COA, or POA.

2\. Provide declaration/bylaws/rules.

3\. Confirm whether notice and hearing occurred.

Suggested next step:

Do not send enforcement letter until document authority is confirmed.

This is extremely aligned with your JanumiCode philosophy.

# 7\. Legal “tests” analogous to software tests

This is one of the most interesting parts.

JanumiCode has tests, linters, structural checks, and review gates. JanumiLegal could have legal equivalents.

## Citation tests

* Does every legal proposition have authority?  
* Is the authority primary, secondary, controlling, or persuasive?  
* Is the quote accurate?  
* Is the authority current?  
* Does the cited authority actually support the proposition?

## Jurisdiction tests

* Is the answer valid for the stated jurisdiction?  
* Are federal/state/local issues separated?  
* Is there a conflict-of-law issue?  
* Does the clause depend on governing law?

## Fact-dependency tests

* Which conclusions depend on which facts?  
* Which facts are unverified?  
* Which facts came from the client?  
* Which facts came from documents?  
* Which facts are inferred?

## Adverse authority tests

* Has contrary authority been searched?  
* Are exceptions identified?  
* Are minority rules identified?  
* Are uncertain areas labeled?

## Contract consistency tests

* Are defined terms used consistently?  
* Do obligations conflict?  
* Are remedies aligned?  
* Are notice periods consistent?  
* Are dates and party names consistent?  
* Are cross-references valid?  
* Are survival clauses complete?

## Privilege tests

* Does this include attorney mental impressions?  
* Should this be marked work product?  
* Is this safe to send externally?  
* Is there client confidential information?

## Unauthorized practice of law tests

* Is the system giving legal advice directly to a non-lawyer?  
* Is a licensed attorney required before release?  
* Is the jurisdiction known?  
* Is the user acting as attorney, legal staff, business user, or consumer?

That last one is critical.

# 12\. The legal version of “too clever by half”

* invents an elegant but impractical legal theory,  
* overfits to a narrow clause and misses business context,  
* cites weak authority as if it were controlling,  
* gives a technically plausible answer without checking exceptions,  
* drafts aggressive language that creates negotiation blowback,  
* assumes litigation posture when the client needs relationship preservation,  
* turns a compliance issue into a theoretical law-school answer,  
* or “solves” a problem by creating a new legal risk.

So JanumiLegal should include a practicality and judgment review agent.

Review the proposed legal output for over-engineering, excessive cleverness,

unnecessary aggressiveness, unsupported legal nuance, and mismatch with the

client’s business objective. Prefer durable, reviewable, conventional legal

reasoning unless a more complex approach is clearly justified.

That maps directly from your JanumiCode philosophy.

# 1\. Menu \+ Mirror in the legal frame

## Mirror

The mirror protocol reflects back what the system believes the human wants.

In code, that might be:

“You want to fix the auth bug without changing the session architecture.”

In legal work, it becomes:

“You want to review this vendor agreement as the customer, with a conservative risk posture, focusing on data security, indemnity, termination rights, and liability exposure.”

The mirror is crucial because legal work is full of hidden assumptions.

For example, the user says:

“Review this contract.”

That is legally under-specified. The system needs to mirror what it inferred:

I understand the task as:

\- You are reviewing this agreement on behalf of the customer.

\- The goal is to identify unacceptable business/legal risk.

\- The output should be a redline-ready issue list, not a full legal memo.

\- I should focus on negotiation posture, not litigation analysis.

\- Attorney review is required before external use.

The mirror prevents the system from doing the wrong kind of legal work.

## Menu

The menu gives the user structured choices instead of asking open-ended questions.

In legal work, menus are especially important because non-lawyers may not know what legal dimensions matter.

Instead of asking:

“What kind of review do you want?”

The system offers:

Choose review posture:

1\. Business-first review

  Focus on cost, flexibility, operational burden, and negotiation leverage.

2\. Legal-risk review

  Focus on liability, indemnity, governing law, remedies, compliance, and enforceability.

3\. Fast red-flag review

  Identify only major deal blockers.

4\. Attorney-prep review

  Produce a structured packet for counsel with questions and suggested edits.

5\. Compliance-mapping review

  Map clauses to operational obligations and controls.

The menu converts vague intent into governed intent.

## Menu \+ Mirror together

The pattern is:

Mirror what the system inferred.

Offer menus where intent is ambiguous.

Record the user’s selection.

Use that selection as a governing constraint.

Example:

Mirror:

You appear to want a buyer-side SaaS contract review, not a neutral summary.

Menu:

Which posture should govern the review?

A. Aggressive negotiation

B. Balanced commercial compromise

C. Conservative legal-risk minimization

D. Fast red-flag triage

User selects:

C

Governed Stream records:

Risk posture \= conservative legal-risk minimization

That selection becomes binding context for downstream agents.

# 2\. Bloom and Prune in the legal frame

Bloom and Prune is how the system explores legal possibility without letting the final output become diffuse, speculative, or over-engineered.

## Bloom

Bloom means deliberately expanding the search space.

In legal work, the bloom phase asks:

* What legal issues could be implicated?  
* What jurisdictions might matter?  
* What facts are missing?  
* What statutes, regulations, cases, contracts, policies, or governing documents might apply?  
* What risks could arise?  
* What counterarguments exist?  
* What negotiation positions are possible?  
* What operational obligations follow?  
* What attorney review decisions are needed?

For a contract review, bloom might produce:

Potential issue universe:

\- Indemnity

\- Limitation of liability

\- Data security

\- Confidentiality

\- IP ownership

\- Audit rights

\- Termination

\- Renewal

\- Payment timing

\- Late fees

\- Assignment

\- Governing law

\- Venue

\- Insurance

\- Service levels

\- Subprocessors

\- Regulatory compliance

\- Dispute resolution

\- Notice mechanics

\- Survival clauses

For an HOA governance question, bloom might produce:

Potential issue universe:

\- Governing document authority

\- Statutory authority

\- Board rulemaking power

\- Notice requirements

\- Hearing requirements

\- Fine schedule

\- Selective enforcement

\- Due process

\- Architectural review authority

\- Records access

\- Owner voting rights

\- Open meeting rules

\- Assessment authority

\- Collection procedure

Bloom is intentionally expansive.

But legal work cannot stop there.

## Prune

Prune means narrowing the expanded universe to what is relevant, supported, safe, and aligned with the client objective.

Pruning criteria might include:

Prune because:

\- Not relevant to the stated jurisdiction

\- Not relevant to the client’s role

\- Not material to the business objective

\- Unsupported by available facts

\- Requires attorney judgment

\- Too speculative

\- Too aggressive for selected posture

\- Not worth negotiating

\- Duplicative of another issue

\- Outside scope

\- Unsafe for non-lawyer release

Example:

Bloom found possible antitrust, employment, privacy, IP, and tax issues.

Prune result:

\- Privacy: in scope because agreement includes customer data.

\- IP: in scope because agreement includes software deliverables.

\- Tax: out of scope because no tax provisions or cross-border facts were provided.

\- Employment: out of scope because no personnel classification issue appears.

\- Antitrust: out of scope absent exclusivity, market allocation, or pricing facts.

This prevents the legal AI from producing a law-school-style issue dump.

## Bloom and Prune as legal judgment scaffolding

The legal value is not just “think of more issues.” It is:

Generate the issue universe.

Explain why each issue survives or is removed.

Make pruning decisions visible.

Escalate uncertain pruning decisions.

That is a major governance advantage.

# 3\. Recursive decomposition in the legal frame

Once the system has pruned to the relevant path, it recursively decomposes the work into smaller legal tasks.

In code, recursive decomposition might break a feature into:

Data model → API → service logic → UI → tests → telemetry

In legal work, recursive decomposition breaks a matter into:

Matter objective

   → legal domains

       → issues

           → elements

               → required facts

                   → supporting evidence

                       → authorities

                           → draft language

                               → verification checks

                                   → attorney decisions

For example:

Task:

Review vendor SaaS agreement.

Decomposition:

1\. Determine role and posture.

2\. Extract contract structure.

3\. Identify high-risk clauses.

4\. Review each clause by risk domain.

5\. Generate issue-specific recommendations.

6\. Produce redlines or fallback positions.

7\. Verify consistency across contract.

8\. Prepare attorney review packet.

Then each subtask decomposes further.

Example: limitation of liability review

Limitation of liability

   → Identify cap amount

   → Identify excluded damages

   → Identify carveouts

   → Compare to playbook

   → Check interaction with indemnity

   → Check interaction with confidentiality

   → Check interaction with data breach obligations

   → Identify business impact

   → Draft preferred position

# Product principles

## 1\. Lens-selected determinism

Every supported legal task must run through a predefined legal lens.

A lens defines:

\- task domain,

\- required intake fields,

\- state machine,

\- state order,

\- allowed branches,

\- output schemas,

\- verification gates,

\- escalation triggers,

\- attorney-review policy,

\- final artifact types.

The lens is not a persona. It is an executable workflow protocol.

## 2\. Bounded LLM cognition

LLMs should be used only inside states where deterministic software cannot reasonably perform the task alone.

Examples:

Appropriate LLM tasks:

\- “Extract all termination provisions from this contract.”

\- “Classify each clause against this risk taxonomy.”

\- “Summarize the factual timeline from these documents.”

\- “Draft a candidate redline using the selected fallback position.”

\- “Identify internal inconsistencies between these provisions.”

Inappropriate LLM tasks:

\- “Decide how to handle this matter.”

\- “Decide whether enough review has occurred.”

\- “Decide whether attorney review is required.”

\- “Decide whether this can be sent externally.”

\- “Invent a custom workflow.”

## 3\. Required states must run

If a lens runs, required states run.

For example, a Contract Review Lens cannot skip:

\- intake normalization,

\- client-role capture,

\- review-mode selection,

\- clause extraction,

\- issue-domain bloom,

\- issue-domain prune,

\- clause-risk packet generation,

\- consistency verification,

\- attorney-review determination,

\- final release-status assignment.

Completeness is enforced procedurally.

## 4\. No silent pruning

When the system removes an issue, risk, clause, authority, or workflow branch, it must record the reason.

Retain / remove / defer / escalate

Reason

Missing facts

Review requirement

This prevents shallow “looks fine” behavior.

## 5\. Attorney authority is explicit

The product must distinguish:

AI-generated draft

Attorney-review packet

Attorney-approved work product

Client-facing communication

External-use artifact

Internal-only analysis

The AI system never silently converts draft legal reasoning into approved legal advice.

## 6\. Release safety is machine-enforced

A final artifact must carry a release status:

internal\_draft

attorney\_review\_required

business\_review\_required

client\_ready\_after\_approval

external\_release\_blocked

insufficient\_information

The LLM does not decide this freely. The state machine and policy gates determine it.

# Restated product requirements

## PRD section: Product objective

JanumiLegal shall provide a governed legal AI workflow platform that transforms underspecified legal/business requests into deterministic, lens-specific workflows that produce structured legal work packets, verification records, escalation items, and attorney-reviewable artifacts.

The system shall minimize open-ended LLM judgment by encoding legal workflow structure into state machines. LLMs shall be used only as bounded executors within individual states, with all outputs constrained by schemas, validation rules, and explicit review policies.

# The most important restatement

The product is not:

“An AI legal assistant that reasons through matters.”

It is:

A deterministic legal matter-processing system that uses LLMs as constrained cognitive components inside versioned, lens-specific state machines.

That is the precise JanumiLegal restatement.

The product requirements should therefore prioritize:

1\. lens definitions,

2\. state-machine definitions,

3\. state contracts,

4\. schema validation,

5\. artifact contracts,

6\. verification gates,

7\. release policies,

8\. attorney-review enforcement,

9\. governed-stream traceability,

10\. bounded agent execution.

Those are the product. The legal agents are implementation details.

What legal benchmarks for AI systems are available that might help validate the performance and quality of this JanumiLegal approach?

I’ll focus on benchmarks that test legal reasoning, retrieval/citation behavior, contract review, hallucination resistance, and end-to-end workflow quality—then map which ones actually validate JanumiLegal’s state-machine approach.  
Thought for 17s

Yes. There are several useful legal AI benchmarks, but none of them fully validates the JanumiLegal thesis by itself, because JanumiLegal is not just a model. It is a lens-selected, deterministic state-machine system with bounded LLM execution.

So the right evaluation strategy is:

Use public legal AI benchmarks to validate bounded state functions.

Use custom JanumiLegal harness benchmarks to validate workflow completeness, state coverage, escalation behavior, and attorney-review readiness.

## 1\. Most relevant public benchmarks

### A. LegalBench

Best for: legal reasoning task coverage.

LegalBench is one of the most important general legal reasoning benchmarks. It contains 162 tasks across six types of legal reasoning, built collaboratively with legal professionals and evaluated across commercial and open-source LLMs. It is useful for testing whether the LLMs used inside JanumiLegal states can perform bounded legal-reasoning subtasks. 

For JanumiLegal, LegalBench is useful for states like:

Issue classification

Rule application

Legal text interpretation

Entailment-like reasoning

Statutory interpretation

Definition matching

But LegalBench does not validate your full product architecture. It mostly evaluates model capability, not whether a deterministic state machine enforced required legal workflow steps.  
---

### B. LexGLUE

Best for: baseline legal NLP/NLU capability.

LexGLUE is a standardized benchmark for English legal language understanding across multiple legal NLU tasks. It is older, but still useful for checking whether base models or specialized legal models perform well on core classification and understanding tasks. 

For JanumiLegal, LexGLUE maps to lower-level bounded states such as:

Document classification

Legal text classification

Clause/topic classification

Case outcome-style classification

Statutory/document understanding

It is less useful for validating JanumiLegal’s state-machine doctrine, because it does not evaluate workflow completeness, release gating, or attorney-review packet quality.  
---

### C. CUAD — Contract Understanding Atticus Dataset

Best for: contract review extraction and clause identification.

CUAD is highly relevant to a Contract Review Lens. It contains 510 commercial contracts, 13,000+ expert labels, and 41 clause types, with annotations created under lawyer supervision. 

For JanumiLegal, CUAD can validate states such as:

ContractStructureExtract

ClauseDomainBloom

ClauseDomainPrune

ClauseRiskPacketGenerate

ClauseReferenceVerification

It is especially good for measuring whether your bounded extraction/classification agents can reliably identify clauses like:

Governing law

Change of control

Anti-assignment

Indemnity

Limitation of liability

Exclusivity

Most favored nation

Non-compete

Termination

IP ownership

CUAD should be one of the first benchmarks used for the Contract Review Lens MVP.  
---

### D. MAUD — Merger Agreement Understanding Dataset

Best for: complex M\&A agreement review.

MAUD contains 152 merger agreements, 47,000+ labels, and 92 questions mapped to deal points from the ABA Public Target Deal Points Study. 

For JanumiLegal, MAUD is useful if you eventually support an M\&A or transaction-specific lens. It can validate:

Deal point extraction

Agreement-specific question answering

Defined-term reasoning

Clause-level provision mapping

Transaction document review

It is probably not an MVP benchmark unless JanumiLegal targets M\&A work early.  
---

### E. ACORD — Atticus Clause Retrieval Dataset

Best for: contract drafting and precedent-clause retrieval.

ACORD is extremely relevant if JanumiLegal supports contract drafting or playbook-based redline generation. It includes 126,000+ expert-rated query-clause pairs, 114 lawyer-written queries, and expert ratings from 1 to 5 stars. It was accepted at ACL 2025\. 

For JanumiLegal, ACORD maps well to states like:

PrecedentClauseRetrieve

FallbackClauseRetrieve

RedlineCandidateGenerate

PlaybookAlignmentCheck

ClauseQualityRank

This benchmark is especially relevant to your deterministic state-machine approach because clause retrieval should be treated as a bounded state, not an open-ended drafting decision.  
---

### F. LegalBench-RAG

Best for: precise legal retrieval.

LegalBench-RAG is designed to evaluate the retrieval component of legal RAG systems. It focuses on retrieving minimal, highly relevant legal text segments, not merely broad documents. The dataset contains 6,858 query-answer pairs over a legal corpus of more than 79 million characters, annotated by legal experts. 

For JanumiLegal, this is highly relevant because your system needs source-grounded states:

AuthorityRetrieve

ProvisionRetrieve

SourceSupportCheck

CitationGroundingCheck

LegalContextPackBuild

This benchmark reinforces a key JanumiLegal design principle: the system should retrieve the right source span before asking an LLM to synthesize.  
---

### G. Legal RAG Bench

Best for: end-to-end legal RAG evaluation and error decomposition.

Legal RAG Bench is a newer benchmark focused on end-to-end legal RAG performance. It uses 4,876 passages from the Victorian Criminal Charge Book and 100 complex hand-crafted questions requiring criminal law/procedure expertise. It also proposes a hierarchical error decomposition framework to separate retrieval failures, reasoning failures, and hallucination failures. 

This is conceptually very aligned with JanumiLegal because your product should not merely score final answers. It should decompose failures by state:

Did retrieval fail?

Did synthesis fail?

Did verification fail?

Did release gating fail?

Did the wrong lens run?

Did pruning remove a necessary issue?

Even though Legal RAG Bench is jurisdiction/domain-specific, its evaluation methodology is directly relevant.  
---

### H. COLIEE

Best for: legal retrieval, entailment, and legal information extraction.

COLIEE is an established legal informatics competition. COLIEE 2025 included case-law retrieval, case-law entailment, statute-law retrieval, statute-law entailment/question answering, and a pilot tort prediction/rationale extraction task. 

For JanumiLegal, COLIEE is useful for validating states such as:

CaseLawRetrieve

StatuteRetrieve

EntailmentCheck

RationaleExtract

AdverseAuthoritySearch

COLIEE is particularly useful if JanumiLegal later supports litigation, statutory interpretation, or legal research memo lenses.  
---

### I. Stanford legal hallucination benchmarks / studies

Best for: hallucination resistance and citation reliability.

Stanford RegLab and collaborators published important empirical work on legal hallucinations. One study found that public-facing LLMs hallucinated frequently on specific, verifiable federal case questions, with reported hallucination rates from 58% for ChatGPT-4 to higher rates for other models. 

A later preregistered evaluation of AI-driven legal research tools found that proprietary RAG-based legal research products reduced but did not eliminate hallucinations; LexisNexis and Thomson Reuters tools were reported to hallucinate between 17% and 33% of the time. 

For JanumiLegal, these are not just benchmarks; they justify the architecture. They support the design rule that the system must not let LLMs freely generate legal citations or final legal claims without verification gates.  
---

### J. PLawBench

Best for: practical legal-work scenarios and rubric-based evaluation.

PLawBench, published in 2026, is designed to evaluate LLMs in realistic legal practice scenarios. It contains 850 questions across 13 practical legal scenarios with about 12,500 expert-designed rubric items, covering public legal consultation, case analysis, and legal document generation. 

This is one of the more relevant benchmarks for JanumiLegal’s long-term vision because it uses finer-grained rubrics rather than just answer correctness. Still, you would need to adapt it because JanumiLegal’s claim is not “the model gives a good answer”; it is “the workflow produces a complete, reviewable legal work packet.”

# 28\. UX principle: never let prose hide workflow state

Legal AI tools often produce polished prose that hides uncertainty.

JanumiLegal should avoid that.

A memo or draft should show inline status markers:

\[Verified Source\]

\[Assumption\]

\[Attorney Review Required\]

\[Unsupported Claim — Blocked\]

\[Jurisdiction Unknown\]

\[External Release Blocked\]

Example:

The association may be able to impose a fine \[Attorney Review Required\], but fine authority was not confirmed in the uploaded governing documents \[Source Gap\].

That is much safer than smooth prose.

# 29\. UX principle: legal users need “why not” views

Deferred questions

Blocked artifacts

Unselected strategies

Rejected redlines

Attorney modifications

This is critical for defensibility.

# 30\. Concrete MVP UI set

For a first JanumiLegal VS Code extension, I would build these seven views.

## 1\. Matter Dashboard

Shows lens, matter type, current state, release status, and blockers.

## 2\. Lens State Machine

Shows required states, completion, validation failures, and transitions.

## 3\. Source / Document Inventory

Shows uploaded legal documents, their roles, parsed status, and linked issues.

## 4\. Issue Tree \+ Pruning Log

Shows bloomed issues, retained/deferred/removed/escalated decisions, and reasons.

## 5\. Risk Register

Shows structured legal/business risks with severity, sources, recommendations, and review status.

## 6\. Attorney Review Packet

Shows decisions needed, open questions, sources, risks, proposed positions, and blockers.

## 7\. Release Gate

Shows whether the artifact is safe for internal use, attorney review, client use, or external release.

That would give you the legal equivalent of JanumiCode’s governed stream \+ architecture decomposition \+ requirements tree.
