Your realization is correct **where you used “canonical” to mean “the governing body of principles by which Janumi concepts should be understood and applied.”** That is **doctrinal**.

But the two words are not points on one simple strength continuum:

* **Canonical** concerns **recognition, identity, and reference**: which version, form, vocabulary, or artifact is accepted as the standard one?  
* **Doctrinal** concerns **principles, interpretation, and judgment**: what system of ideas should guide reasoning and action?

A document can therefore be both: **the canonical statement of Janumi doctrine**.

## **The semantic landscape**

The related words fall along several intersecting continuums.

### **1\. What a text or artifact does**

Descriptive  
    ↓  
Explanatory  
    ↓  
Interpretive  
    ↓  
Doctrinal  
    ↓  
Normative  
    ↓  
Prescriptive  
    ↓  
Mandatory / Binding  
    ↓  
Executable / Enforced

| Term | Function |
| ----- | ----- |
| **Descriptive** | Records what exists or what people currently do. |
| **Explanatory** | Explains why something exists or how it works. |
| **Interpretive** | Provides a way to understand ambiguous concepts or situations. |
| **Doctrinal** | Establishes a coherent body of principles for reasoning, judgment, and action. |
| **Normative** | States what ought to be true or what constitutes conformance. |
| **Prescriptive** | Directs a particular course of action or method. |
| **Mandatory** | Requires compliance rather than merely recommending it. |
| **Binding** | Carries an obligation within a defined governance or legal system. |
| **Executable** | Is mechanically enforced through schemas, controls, validators, invariants, or code. |

**Doctrinal** therefore sits between pure interpretation and concrete prescription. Doctrine tells practitioners how to think and exercise judgment; it does not necessarily dictate every step.

For example:

Shape Engineering doctrine says that agentic power should be shaped through explicit work architecture, decomposition, feedback, evidence, and assurance.

That doctrine might then produce normative requirements, specifications, and executable validators.

### **2\. The recognition or reference status of something**

Illustrative  
    ↓  
Representative  
    ↓  
Reference  
    ↓  
Preferred  
    ↓  
Canonical  
    ↓  
Definitive

This is the continuum on which **canonical** primarily belongs.

| Term | Meaning |
| ----- | ----- |
| **Illustrative** | One example intended to clarify an idea. |
| **Representative** | A typical or broadly representative example. |
| **Reference** | An example or model intended to be consulted by others. |
| **Preferred** | Recommended over alternatives, though alternatives remain legitimate. |
| **Canonical** | Recognized as the standard, normalized, or accepted representation among alternatives. |
| **Definitive** | Intended to settle or comprehensively answer the matter. |

These are not always strict levels of authority. A canonical form can emerge informally, while an official document can fail to become the practically canonical reference.

Good uses of **canonical** in Janumi would include:

* the **canonical definition** of a Professional Work Unit;  
* the **canonical Janumi vocabulary**;  
* the **canonical serialized representation** of a PWA;  
* the **canonical ontology** for professional work;  
* the **canonical version** of a doctrinal document;  
* a **canonical decomposition**, where several equivalent decompositions must be normalized into one form.

Canonical does **not**, by itself, mean governing, mandatory, philosophically foundational, or correct.

Conventional / De facto

Adopted

    ↓  
Authoritative

| **Conventional** | Accepted through practice or custom. |
| **De facto** | Functions as the standard in practice, whether formally adopted or not. |
| **Adopted** | Has been formally accepted by the relevant body. |
| **Official** | Issued or endorsed by the responsible institution. |

| **Controlling** | Takes precedence when sources or requirements conflict. |
| **Constitutional** | Establishes the highest-order structure, authorities, boundaries, and constraints of the system. |

Again, these adjectives can combine:

* an **official doctrinal publication**;  
* an **authoritative interpretation**;  
* a **canonical but nonbinding reference model**;  
* a **controlling normative specification**;  
* a **constitutional invariant**.

## **The governing stack**

For Janumi, the most useful hierarchy is probably not a word-strength continuum but a **governance and operationalization stack**:

Vision / Worldview / Thesis  
            ↓  
Values, Axioms, and First Principles  
            ↓  
Constitution / Charter  
            ↓  
Doctrine  
            ↓  
Policy  
            ↓  
Standards  
            ↓  
Specifications  
            ↓  
Procedures and Playbooks  
            ↓  
Controls, Invariants, and Validators

### **Vision, worldview, or thesis**

This explains the reality Janumi believes it is addressing and the future it seeks to create.

Examples include:

* humanity converts underspecified intent into consequential reality through professional capability;  
* AI should amplify organized professional cognition rather than merely automate isolated tasks;  
* civilizational capability depends on preserving, coordinating, and assuring accumulated professional knowledge.

This layer answers:

What do we believe is happening, and what are we trying to make possible?

### **Values, axioms, tenets, and principles**

These are related but distinguishable:

* A **value** identifies a preferred end: human agency, coherence, safety, traceability.  
* An **axiom** is a premise accepted within the framework.  
* A **tenet** is a central commitment or belief.  
* A **principle** is a general rule used to guide decisions.  
* A **first principle** is treated as foundational rather than derived from lower-level rules.

Examples:

* consequential work requires assurance proportional to consequence;  
* underspecified intent must not silently become unexamined implementation assumptions;  
* professional outputs should preserve traceability to originating intent.

### **Constitution or charter**

A **charter** establishes purpose, scope, powers, and responsibilities. A **constitution** establishes the highest-order rules and limitations governing the system.

For Janumi, a conceptual constitution might determine:

* what authority human users retain;  
* what agents may and may not autonomously decide;  
* what evidence must accompany consequential decisions;  
* which invariants no PWA may override;  
* how conflicts among policies, agents, validators, and users are resolved;  
* what constitutes legitimate professional work within the platform.

The constitution is not merely doctrine. It establishes the governing order within which doctrine operates.

### **Doctrine**

Doctrine is an organized body of principles used to guide interpretation and action across recurring classes of situations.

It answers:

How should Janumi practitioners and systems reason when the correct action cannot be reduced to one fixed rule?

Possible doctrinal bodies include:

* **Shape Engineering Doctrine**  
* **Harness Engineering Doctrine**  
* **Assurance Engineering Doctrine**  
* **Professional Work Architecture Doctrine**  
* **Human–Agent Authority Doctrine**  
* **Evidence and Traceability Doctrine**  
* **Doctrine for Continuous AI Reconciliation**

Doctrine is especially appropriate where judgment remains necessary. It provides a disciplined way to reason without pretending that every case can be decided through a static procedure.

### **Policy**

Policy converts principles and doctrine into institutional decisions.

Examples:

* all high-consequence PWUs require independent validation;  
* tenant policies may strengthen but not weaken platform constitutional controls;  
* production changes require evidence from specified verification stages;  
* certain decisions require human authorization.

Doctrine explains the logic; policy states what the organization has decided.

### **Standard**

A standard defines common criteria, interfaces, classifications, or quality expectations.

Examples:

* PWA interoperability standard;  
* PWU evidence completeness standard;  
* validator result classification standard;  
* professional provenance standard.

A standard may be voluntary in one environment and mandatory in another. Its force depends on how it is adopted.

### **Specification**

A specification states exact, testable requirements.

Examples:

* PWA metamodel specification;  
* PWU lifecycle specification;  
* validator interface specification;  
* evidence envelope schema;  
* RPH execution semantics.

Specifications are where terms such as **MUST**, **SHOULD**, and **MAY** become useful.

### **Procedure or playbook**

A procedure tells actors what sequence to follow. A playbook may allow more situational discretion.

Examples:

* procedure for escalating unresolved validator disagreement;  
* playbook for assumption surfacing;  
* procedure for changing tactics after repeated failure;  
* incident-response playbook for PWA execution failures.

### **Controls, invariants, and validators**

This is where institutional intent becomes executable.

* A **control** reduces or manages a risk.  
* An **invariant** defines a condition that must remain true.  
* A **validator** evaluates whether an artifact or action satisfies relevant criteria.  
* A **gate** prevents progression until specified conditions are met.  
* A **monitor** observes whether conditions remain within an acceptable operating envelope.

This is where doctrine, policy, and specifications become part of the actual control system rather than remaining prose.

## **Nearby words that are easily confused**

| Word | Distinguishing question |
| ----- | ----- |
| **Foundational** | Is it necessary to the rest of the framework? |
| **Canonical** | Is this the recognized form or version? |
| **Official** | Has the institution formally issued or adopted it? |
| **Authoritative** | Does this source have standing to settle interpretation? |
| **Definitive** | Is it intended to provide the complete or final account? |
| **Doctrinal** | Does it articulate governing principles for judgment and action? |
| **Normative** | Does it state what ought or must be true? |
| **Prescriptive** | Does it direct what someone should do? |
| **Binding** | Is compliance obligatory? |
| **Controlling** | Does it prevail when requirements conflict? |
| **Constitutional** | Does it establish the governing order and highest constraints? |
| **Orthodox** | Does it conform to accepted doctrine? |
| **Dogmatic** | Is it asserted rigidly and treated as beyond challenge? |

Two especially useful distinctions are:

**Doctrine versus orthodoxy:** Doctrine is the body of principles; orthodoxy is conformity with the accepted doctrine.

**Doctrine versus dogma:** Doctrine can guide reasoned interpretation and evolve through evidence. Dogma implies that a proposition is treated as incontrovertible, often with a negative connotation.

## **The old religious analogy is surprisingly precise**

The historical relationship among these terms provides a useful mnemonic:

* **Canon** answers: *Which texts belong to the recognized corpus?*  
* **Doctrine** answers: *What principles do those texts establish?*  
* **Creed** answers: *What are the central beliefs in condensed form?*  
* **Dogma** answers: *Which propositions have been formally fixed?*  
* **Orthodoxy** answers: *What counts as conformity with accepted doctrine?*

Transferred into Janumi:

* The **canon** is the recognized corpus of Janumi artifacts.  
* The **doctrine** is the system of principles they articulate.  
* The **constitution** establishes the highest governing commitments.  
* The **normative specifications** state conformance requirements.  
* The **validators and invariants** enforce selected requirements.

**“Applied” and “practice” belong to a different dimension from “canonical,” “authoritative,” “doctrinal,” and “normative.”**

* **Canonical** describes an artifact’s **recognized status**.  
* **Doctrinal** describes its **role in guiding thought and judgment**.

They can therefore stack rather than replace one another:

**the canonical statement of a doctrine governing professional practice**

or:

**an applied interpretation of the doctrine for software engineering practice**

# **“Applied” is an orientation, not a body of rules**

**Applied** is normally an adjective. It says that something abstract is being directed toward practical use.

Theory → Applied theory  
Science → Applied science  
Research → Applied research  
Mathematics → Applied mathematics  
Ethics → Applied ethics  
Doctrine → Applied doctrine

The contrast is usually:

Fundamental / Pure / Theoretical  
                 versus  
Applied / Problem-directed / Use-oriented

For example:

* **Pure mathematics** develops mathematical knowledge without requiring an immediate application.  
* **Applied mathematics** uses mathematical structures to model and solve practical problems.  
* **Basic research** seeks general knowledge.  
* **Applied research** seeks knowledge relevant to a practical objective.  
* **Normative ethics** develops general accounts of right action.  
* **Applied ethics** examines how those accounts bear on medicine, engineering, warfare, business, and other concrete domains.

“Applied” therefore means something like:

**translated, adapted, or directed toward solving a practical class of problems**

It does not necessarily mean that action is currently being performed. An applied framework can still exist entirely as a written framework.

## **Applied doctrine**

Although less common than “applied science” or “applied ethics,” **applied doctrine** is conceptually valid.

You could distinguish:

* **General doctrine:** consequential agentic work requires evidence proportional to risk.  
* **Applied doctrine for software engineering:** production code changes require evidence from tests, static analysis, security review, and deployment observation.  
* **Applied doctrine for medicine:** consequential treatment recommendations require appropriate clinical evidence, patient-specific assessment, and professional oversight.

The applied form retains the governing principle but adapts it to the ontology, risks, evidence, actors, and constraints of a particular domain.

# **“Practice” is enacted competence**

**Practice** is richer and more ambiguous. In the present discussion, its most important meaning is:

**the organized exercise of a profession or discipline in real situations**

Thus:

* the practice of medicine;  
* the practice of law;  
* engineering practice;  
* architectural practice;  
* clinical practice;  
* professional practice.

Practice is not merely “using theory.” It normally includes:

* situated judgment;  
* tacit knowledge;  
* accumulated experience;  
* professional norms;  
* techniques and routines;  
* ethical obligations;  
* responsibility for outcomes;  
* adaptation to circumstances;  
* interaction with clients, patients, colleagues, institutions, and regulators.

That is why **medical practice** is idiomatic and meaningful in a way that **applied medicine** often is not.

Medicine is not merely medical science applied to a patient. Medical practice includes the professional act of deciding:

* what evidence is relevant;  
* what uncertainty is acceptable;  
* what the patient values;  
* whether intervention is warranted;  
* which risks are tolerable;  
* when to refer or escalate;  
* how to communicate;  
* how to monitor and revise the decision.

So:

Medical science  
      ↓  
Clinical knowledge and doctrine  
      ↓  
Clinical methods and guidelines  
      ↓  
Medical practice  
      ↓  
Care of this particular patient

## **The several meanings of “practice”**

“Practice” can refer to at least five different things.

### **1\. The exercise of a profession**

She practices medicine.

Engineering practice requires professional judgment.

This is the primary meaning relevant here.

### **2\. A customary way of working**

It is standard practice to conduct an independent design review.

Here, a practice is a recurrent pattern of action.

### **3\. A recognized technique or organizational pattern**

Continuous integration is a software engineering practice.

This means a reusable, teachable way of working—not necessarily a rigid procedure.

### **4\. A professional organization or business**

He joined a medical practice.

She founded an architectural practice.

Here, “practice” means the organized entity through which the profession is exercised.

### **5\. Rehearsal or repetition for skill development**

Deliberate practice improves performance.

This is a separate meaning and is usually clear from context.

# **Practice versus doctrine**

The relationship can be stated compactly:

**Doctrine guides practice; practice interprets, tests, and evolves doctrine.**

Doctrine is not simply a description of what practitioners already do. It establishes the principles by which professional action should be understood and judged.

Practice, however, is where those principles encounter reality.

Doctrine  
   ↓ guides  
Practice  
   ↓ generates experience and evidence  
Doctrinal refinement

This reciprocal relationship matters. Without doctrine, practice may become fragmented, customary, or locally expedient. Without practice, doctrine may become detached, brittle, or idealized.

A mature profession therefore has a loop:

Knowledge  
   ↓  
Doctrine  
   ↓  
Practice  
   ↓  
Evidence and experience  
   ↓  
Reflection  
   ↓  
Revised knowledge and doctrine

The word **praxis** is sometimes used for this especially reflective union of theory and action.

## **Praxis**

**Praxis** does not merely mean practical activity. It usually suggests:

informed action in which theory shapes practice and experience from practice reshapes understanding.

For Janumi, “praxis” could be valuable when discussing the learning loop by which accumulated professional reasoning becomes operational, is exercised, is observed, and is revised.

But it is more philosophical and less immediately accessible than “professional practice.”

# **Practice versus procedure**

A practice is usually broader and more judgment-dependent than a procedure.

Doctrine  
   ↓  
Practice  
   ↓  
Method  
   ↓  
Procedure  
   ↓  
Action

For example:

* **Doctrine:** assumptions affecting consequential decisions must be exposed and examined.  
* **Practice:** assumption surfacing.  
* **Method:** structured assumption elicitation and challenge.  
* **Procedure:** identify claims, classify uncertainty, find supporting evidence, score impact, assign disposition.  
* **Action:** challenge assumption A-17 in the current product specification.

A professional **practice** can contain several methods and procedures. It can also require judgment about which method is appropriate.

That distinction is particularly important for agentic systems. Encoding a procedure is relatively straightforward. Encoding professional practice requires representing:

* when the procedure applies;  
* when it does not;  
* what evidence counts;  
* how exceptions are handled;  
* how conflicts are resolved;  
* when judgment or escalation is necessary.

# **Practice versus “best practice”**

“Best practice” is frequently overused.

It often means:

a generally recommended way of working under assumed conditions.

But it can misleadingly imply that one method is universally optimal. More precise alternatives include:

* **accepted practice** — commonly recognized;  
* **standard practice** — ordinarily followed;  
* **leading practice** — associated with advanced practitioners;  
* **recommended practice** — advised but not mandatory;  
* **good practice** — generally sound;  
* **context-appropriate practice** — suitable under specified conditions;  
* **evidence-based practice** — supported by relevant evidence;  
* **professional practice** — part of competent exercise of the profession.

For Janumi, “context-appropriate practice” or “evidence-backed practice” may often be better than “best practice,” because the system is explicitly concerned with situational judgment.

# **Is “applied engineering” redundant?**

To some degree, **yes**, because engineering is already intrinsically directed toward practical realization.

Science is primarily concerned with understanding phenomena. Engineering is primarily concerned with intentionally creating, modifying, operating, or assuring systems under constraints.

That yields the conventional contrast:

Science: What is true, and how does it behave?  
Engineering: What can and should we make, and how can we make it work?

Engineering is therefore already an application-oriented discipline.

But **applied engineering** can still be meaningful where it establishes a contrast inside engineering education or work. It may imply:

* emphasis on implementation rather than foundational analysis;  
* use of established engineering knowledge in operational settings;  
* practical integration of components and technologies;  
* deployment, production, maintenance, or field application;  
* less emphasis on deriving new scientific or mathematical models;  
* stronger emphasis on making known technologies work in context.

The problem is that the phrase does not specify **what it is being contrasted with**.

Possible contrasts include:

Engineering science       ↔ Applied engineering  
Research engineering      ↔ Applied engineering  
Theoretical engineering   ↔ Engineering practice  
Foundational methods      ↔ Field implementation  
Design theory             ↔ Operational realization

Consequently, a more precise term is often preferable:

* **engineering practice**;  
* **applied engineering science**;  
* **implementation engineering**;  
* **field engineering**;  
* **production engineering**;  
* **systems integration engineering**;  
* **operational engineering**;  
* **engineering application**.

Which one is correct depends on what distinction is intended.

## **“Engineering practice” is broader than “applied engineering”**

**Applied engineering** emphasizes using engineering knowledge toward practical objectives.

**Engineering practice** encompasses the professional activity as a whole:

* problem framing;  
* requirements;  
* design;  
* analysis;  
* trade studies;  
* implementation;  
* verification;  
* validation;  
* safety;  
* ethics;  
* communication;  
* configuration control;  
* lifecycle responsibility;  
* professional accountability.

Thus:

Applied engineering emphasizes the practical orientation of the knowledge.

Engineering practice denotes the actual professional exercise of engineering.

# **A four-axis vocabulary model**

The easiest way to avoid treating all these terms as one continuum is to place them on four separate axes.

## **Axis 1: Recognition and authority**

Illustrative → Reference → Preferred → Canonical  
Informal → Official → Authoritative → Controlling

Question:

What status does this artifact have?

## **Axis 2: Intellectual and normative function**

Descriptive → Interpretive → Doctrinal → Normative → Prescriptive

Question:

What does this body of knowledge do?

## **Axis 3: Abstraction to application**

Theoretical → Translational → Applied → Operational

Question:

How close is this knowledge to practical use?

## **Axis 4: Knowledge to enactment**

Principle → Methodology → Practice → Procedure → Execution

Question:

At what level is professional activity represented?

A single thing can have one description on every axis.

For example:

**An authoritative, doctrinal, applied framework for professional engineering practice.**

That phrase means:

* **authoritative** — it has recognized institutional standing;  
* **doctrinal** — it establishes principles for judgment;  
* **applied** — it translates those principles toward practical engineering problems;  
* **practice** — it governs the actual exercise of the profession.
